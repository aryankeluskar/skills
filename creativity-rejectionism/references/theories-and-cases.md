## 1. The two near-universal conditions

Across essentially all theories, a creative thought/behavior must be:
- **Novel** (original), and
- **Valuable** (useful / adaptive).

Feist (1998): creativity researchers have been "nearly unanimous" that creative thought or behavior must be both novel-original and useful-adaptive. Almost everyone then adds a **third criterion**, partly to exclude lucky trial-and-error or serendipity (e.g. Goodyear's chance discovery of vulcanized rubber), partly to exclude "dark creativity" (ingenious torture) and non-agentive sources (plate tectonics). The four families below differ mainly in what that third criterion is.

## 2. The four families of creativity theory

A heuristic taxonomy, not an exhaustive partition.

### Family A — Experiential (individuated by the agent's experience)
Creativity requires a particular subjective character.
- **Boden (1996, 2004, 2010, 2014):** beyond novelty and value, a creative thought/action must be **surprising**.
- **Insight / "aha!" tradition (Hutchinson 1941; Kounios & Beeman 2009):** creativity is tied to the felt moment of insight. This connects directly to the phenomenology problem for testing non-verbal subjects (Shettleworth 2012: the "acid test" of human insight is its distinctive phenomenology).

### Family B — Capacity / agency (individuated by psychological capacities exercised)
- **Gaut (2010):** novelty and value must be supplemented by **flair** — "a relevant purpose… some degree of understanding… a degree of judgement… and an evaluative ability directed to the task at hand."
- **Paul & Stokes (2018):** **intentional agency** is criterial for creative action.

### Family C — Spontaneity (creativity as *unlike* ordinary goal-directed behavior)
- **Kronfeldner (2009, 2018):** **spontaneity** is critical — "a certain independence from the intentional control and the previously acquired knowledge of the person whose creativity is at issue."
- **Amabile (1996):** creative processes operate as **heuristics** for problem-solving rather than explicitly represented solutions.
- **Koestler (1964):** **bisociation** — bringing together "two self-consistent but habitually incompatible frames of reference."

### Family D — Process / stage (a temporal sequence of cognitive steps)
- **Four-step (Wallas 1926; Hadamard 1954, from Poincaré):** preparation → incubation → inspiration → verification.
- **Two-step blind-variation (Campbell 1960; Simonton 2009):** randomized/"blind" variation → selective retention of the most valuable ideas.
- **Geneplore (Finke et al. 1992; Ward et al. 1999):** a generate phase → an explore/implement phase.

**Why the taxonomy matters for appraisal:** the same animal/AI behavior can pass under one family and fail under another. A behavior produced by blind variation + selection (Family D) may lack the surprise phenomenology (Family A) or the intentional flair (Family B). Always name the family a claim depends on.

## 3. Putative cases of creative intelligence in animals

Shevlin's "highlights reel" (§3), grouped by the capacity they showcase. Each is *prima facie* creative and each is contestable.

### Insight (§3.1)
- **Köhler (1925):** chimps stacked boxes to reach hung bananas, often after a pause of looking — superficially resembling human insight ("standing still and looking… before suddenly hitting upon a solution"). Replicated in chimps (Schiller 1957) and pigeons (Epstein et al. 1984).
- Insight-like problem-solving (success after an impasse involving restructuring/reconceptualization) reported in ravens (Heinrich 1995), rats (Durstewitz et al. 2010), orangutans (Mendes et al. 2007).
- **Contests:** residual role for reinforcement learning even in apparent insight (Taylor et al. 2010); animals often only reach novel solutions when component behaviors are already in repertoire or were reinforced (Epstein et al. 1981); the human "acid test" is a phenomenology you can't probe non-verbally (Shettleworth 2012).

### Improvisation (§3.2)
Defined (Russon et al. 2010) as "on the spot, spontaneous solutions to a task facing the actor."
- Mientian tree frogs using storm drains to amplify mating calls (Tan et al. 2014).
- Great tits piercing milk-bottle lids for cream (Hinde & Fisher 1951).
- Japanese macaques washing sweet potatoes (Kawai 1965).
- **Karen Pryor's "101 things to do with a box" (Pryor et al. 1969; Pryor 1999, 2000):** rough-toothed porpoises rewarded only for *new* actions began generating large numbers of previously unseen behaviors. Procedure since used with other marine mammals and dogs.
- **Contest:** many such behaviors likely began as a chance action, got reinforced, then spread by social learning (great tits) — serendipity, not foresight.

### Physical problem-solving (§3.3)
- **Betty the New Caledonian crow (Weir et al. 2002):** spontaneously bent straight wire into a hook to retrieve food. *Contest:* linked to wild crows' twig-bending (Rutz et al. 2016).
- **Goffin cockatoo "Pipin" (Auersperg 2015):** untrained, dismantled five unfamiliar locks in ~100 min — pulling a pin, unscrewing a bolt, pressing a cylinder through a ring, etc. Cockatoos/keas react appropriately to layout changes, ignoring now-irrelevant locks (Auersperg et al. 2013).
- **Aesop's-fable tasks:** raise water level to reach reward. Orangutans/chimps spit water in (Mendes et al. 2007; Hanus et al. 2011); rooks, Eurasian jays, NC crows drop stones, preferring solid over hollow objects (Bird & Emery 2009b; Jelbert et al. 2014 — "rivalling that of 5–7 year old children"). *Contest:* meta-analysis explains corvid performance via trial-and-error + innate preferences for functional objects (Hennefield et al. 2019).

## 4. The rescue strategies in detail

How each attempts to save creativity-talk, and why Shevlin finds each insufficient for rigorous comparative/AI science.

### Degrees
Creativity comes in degrees (Kronfeldner 2018). Intuitive; lets you avoid a creative/not-creative binary for a species or individual. **Problem:** if still tied to a unified psychological kind, it inherits all the definitional/measurement hazards — graded application of an ill-defined concept is no clearer than binary application.

### Divide and conquer
Decompose creativity into parts.
- **Boden (2004):** combinational / exploratory / transformational creativity.
- **Kaufman & Beghetto (2009):** four-C model — mini-c / little-c / Pro-C / Big-C, tracking different forms of innovating, learning, discovery across individuals and life-stages.
**Problem:** valuable, but be explicit about the goal. Folk-conceptual *analysis* and building *operational tools for cognitive science* are different projects with different methods; conflating them breeds cross-purpose confusion and a needless proliferation of distinctions. Pursued for measurement, this strategy effectively makes reference to "creativity" unnecessary — i.e. it slides into rejectionism.

### Prototype concept
**Chen (2018), via Rosch (1973):** treat creativity not as necessary+sufficient conditions but as governed by **similarity to central exemplars** (great feats of imagination and insight). Accommodates value-ladenness and the lack of researcher agreement. **Problem (Shevlin):** if membership rides on culturally- and context-relative similarity judgments, the concept is **ill-suited to rigorous comparative psychology**. Chen concedes creativity "might well split into a plurality of natural kinds."

### Rejectionism (the recommendation)
- **Weak:** a comparative science of creativity may be possible later; not viable now given open controversies.
- **Strong:** creativity is already the wrong *kind* of concept for comparative psychology — akin to **wit, charisma, good taste** rather than **learning, memory, g-factor**. So the question "is animal/machine X creative?" is not substantive for the field. Replace the umbrella with operationalized constructs (behavioral plasticity, neophilia, exploration, innovation, divergent thinking, insight-as-restructuring), defined stipulatively, with no deference to the folk concept.

**Precedents for the rejectionist move:**
- Episodic memory → **"episodic-like memory"**, operationalized as representations of specific what/when/where (Clayton et al. 2003; Nairne 2015), sidestepping phenomenology.
- Intelligence → **g-factor** (Spearman 1927), abandoning a definitive account of intelligence per se — though g-factor's own status as a natural kind stays contested (Serpico 2018), which is itself a cautionary note.

**The honest residual worry (§5):** this may "move the bump in the carpet" — novelty is scarcely more perspicuous than creativity. Shevlin's reply: stipulation + pluralism still yields two real gains — (1) typologies across many dimensions of behavioral variability without theorizing a shared essence, and (2) no leftover "but is it *really* creative?" question once a behavior satisfies a stipulated operational definition.
